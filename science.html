The Science

How we turn agricultural waste into books that store carbon.

FiberCane Paper

70% Bagasse Fiber
30% Recycled Pulp

Our pages use sugarcane bagasse -- the fibrous waste left after sugar extraction. This material would otherwise be burned. Blended with recycled pulp, it produces paper that's identical in quality to traditional stock.

Forest Conservation

Approximately 4 billion trees are cut for paper each year. By using bagasse instead of wood pulp, we eliminate the need for virgin timber. Forests stay standing as carbon sinks and biodiversity habitats.

Carbon Sequestration

Sugarcane uses C4 photosynthesis to capture CO2 at rates far exceeding most trees -- up to 22 tons of CO2 per hectare per year. When that fiber becomes a book, the carbon stays locked on your shelf for decades instead of being released back into the atmosphere.

Circular Economy

Sugarcane juice becomes sugar or bioethanol fuel
Leftover bagasse becomes FiberCane paper
Recycled bottles become rPET book lamination
Soybean oil becomes ultra low VOC ink

Nothing is wasted. Farmers earn dual revenue from sugar and fiber sales.

Ink & Cover Technology

Ultra Low VOC Soy Ink

Fewer volatile organic compounds, easier recycling, safer for workers.

rPET Library Lamination

Recycled water bottles become durable covers that protect books for 10-15 years.

Designed for Recycling

High-heat delamination lets the plastic separate from paper at end of life.
